Now the new dataset is devoid of any missing values. I can now move on to the analysis portion of the data analysis portion. The reason we took out the rows missing age values, is because later when we calculate the average of ages our results will be consistent with passengers that had their age listed. Say we did not remove these missing values, when we calculate the average age, it will not be representative of the passengers in a certain category, because some passengers in that category will not have their age listed. Later on, when we're figuring out the connections between age and other variables, this inconsistency will also affect the analysis of other variables that also deal with age.

#This stuff is important for reference but the below block of print statements I think shows more perspective.

def print_function(male_survived, female_survived, mcount, fcount, total_alive):
    print("Male survivors: {}".format(male_survived))
    print("Male dead: {}".format(mcount-male_survived))
    print("Female survivors: {}".format(female_survived))
    print("Female dead: {}".format(fcount-female_survived))
    print('\n')

    print("Percentage of total males survived: {}%".format(float(male_survived)/mcount * 100))
    print("Percentage of total females survived: {}%".format(float(female_survived)/fcount * 100))
    print('\n')
    print("Percentage of males in survivors: {}%".format(float(male_survived)/total_alive *100))
    print("Percentage of females in survivors: {}%".format(float(female_survived)/total_alive *100))
    return

#Returns a bar plot with dual bars for each age range for both dead and survived. 
#It takes three arguments --> 2 desired index positions, and a string indicating which gender

def dual_bar_plots(index1, index2, gender):
    ind = np.arange(4)
    width = 0.2

    p1 = plt.bar(ind, [chd.iloc[index1], ya.iloc[index1], ad.iloc[index1], eld.iloc[index1]], width)
    p2 = plt.bar(ind + width, [chd.iloc[index2], ya.iloc[index2], ad.iloc[index2], eld.iloc[index2]], width, color='y')

    plt.xticks((ind+width), ('Children\n(0-18)', 'Young-Adults\n(18-30)', 'Adults\n(31-50)', 'Over 50'))
    plt.legend((p1[0], p2[0]), ('{} Dead'.format(gender), '{} Survived'.format(gender)))
    plt.title('{} Age Ranges - Survived & Dead'.format(gender))
    
    return

Limitations¶

There were lots of missing data in the age and cabin column. Since I was not using the cabin column for my analysis I took it out from the dataset. There were 177 missing values in the age column, and so I created a new dataset that took out all the rows with missing age values. Upon further exploration I found that there were two missing values in the 'Embarked' column. This was a problem because when I called the dataframe method 'dropna' I was removing an additional two rows I didn't have to because of the two values missing in the 'Embarked' column. The new dataset also took this into account and removed this problematic column.

Conclusion¶

The below analysis are but describing tendencies and are in no way definitive predictions. The conclusions that I come up with are but speculations that are backed up by the data, though loosely. I understand that this dataset is but a sample, and as I was analyzing it I made the assumption that this sample is representative of the titanic population.

There were four major indicators that predicted one's survival: female gender, pclass, child, and elderly.

When looking at the data, we see that although males made up about 2/3 of the population, only about 1/3 of them survived. The vice versa happened with females. And it wasn't just one group of females, like children females, but females had at least double the amount of survivors than dead across all the age ranges. Upon looking at how traveling with family affected one's chances of survival I noticed there was a tendency of passengers with lots of parch/sibsp to have a lower chance of survival than their lower parch/sibsp counterparts. I was interested that if I were to introduce pclass with parch/sibsp would I see any other interesting tendencies. I noticed that the higher pclass you were the greater chance you had surviving. Even among those with lots of parch/sibsp, but you were in the upper class, you had a better chance of surviving respective to your parch/sibsp group. Overall the upper class had the most percentage of survivors. The data revealed that across all the passengers (w/ age values):

Percentage of Pclass1 survived: 64.5161290323%
Percentage of Pclass2 survived: 47.9768786127%
Percentage of Pclass3 survived: 23.9436619718%

Not suprisingly, children were taken great care of regardless of gender. Though gender had no effect in children's survival, pclass did. The survival rate for children in pclass 1 and 2 were roughly 91%, but the survival rate for pclass3 children was roughly 37%. You would have thought that the children were taken great care of regardless of their class, but this was not the case. All this classification of pclass makes me think that perhaps the way the cabins were structured based on pclass might have been a possibility instead of the ship captains making the executive decision to take priority those in the upper class, middle, then lower.

Almost all of the the females over 50 survived. Only 12.7% of those males over 50 survived. Maybe they cared more about taking the elderly females over the elderly males.

Percentage of males in those over age fifty: 73.4375%

The female elderly were taken great care for, while the male elderly not so much. Only about 13% of them survived. Specifically out of 47 elderly male, only 6 of them survived. Whereas out of the 17 female elderly, only 16 of them survived. Perhaps since there were so few elderly female that they were easier to locate than the elderly male. And amidst all the choas the elderly male were forgotten because they were too hard to locate. Another interesting thing is that the majority of the elderly folk were in the upper classes. 26 elderly male in the upper class, and 13 elderly female in the upper class. That makes about roughly 60.94% of the elderly population who were in the upper class.
